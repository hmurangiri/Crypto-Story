## Page 25: The Shop Where Crypto Is Bought and Sold

One Saturday afternoon, Amina visited Kamau at his electronics shop.

A customer had just paid for earphones. Kamau entered the sale into a small computer system, printed a receipt, and placed the cash in a drawer.

Amina watched him for a moment.

“People keep telling me to buy crypto on an exchange,” she said. “What is an exchange?”

Kamau pointed toward the busy market outside.

“Think of a market where buyers and sellers meet. A crypto exchange does something similar, but online.”

A **crypto exchange** is a platform where people can buy, sell, or trade crypto assets.

Some exchanges allow users to deposit Kenyan shillings or another national currency. Others only support crypto deposits. Some connect buyers and sellers directly. Others act as the main middleman.

Amina opened an exchange app on Kamau’s phone.

The screen showed many strange pairs:

- BTC/USDT
- ETH/USDT
- BTC/USD
- ETH/BTC

“What do these pairs mean?” she asked.

“A trading pair compares the price of one asset with another.”

In the pair **BTC/USDT**, BTC is the asset being bought or sold. USDT is the asset used to measure and pay for it.

If the price shows 100,000 USDT, it means one bitcoin is being valued at 100,000 units of USDT at that moment.

The exact market price keeps changing because buyers and sellers keep changing their offers.

Amina noticed two large buttons.

One said **Buy**.

The other said **Sell**.

“It looks easy,” she said.

“That is why beginners must be careful,” Kamau replied. “Easy buttons can hide complicated risks.”

An exchange may provide useful services:

- converting cash into crypto,
- converting crypto back into cash,
- matching buyers with sellers,
- storing assets for users,
- showing market prices,
- and offering trading tools.

But using an exchange also means trusting a company.

The company may control the private keys for assets held on the platform. It may freeze withdrawals, request identity documents, suffer a hack, face legal restrictions, or become insolvent.

Amina remembered the earlier lesson:

**Not your keys, not your coins.**

But she also remembered the second half of the lesson.

Self-custody gives control, but also responsibility.

An exchange account may be easier for a beginner. A self-custody wallet may offer more control. Neither removes risk.

Kamau advised her to separate three activities in her mind:

1. **Buying crypto**
2. **Trading crypto**
3. **Storing crypto**

A platform that is convenient for buying may not be the best place for long-term storage.

A wallet that is suitable for long-term storage may not be convenient for frequent trading.

Amina wrote:

> An exchange is a marketplace and service provider. It is not the same thing as the blockchain itself.

If one exchange closes, Bitcoin does not automatically stop.

If one mobile money agent closes, the entire Kenyan shilling does not disappear.

The service and the underlying system are different things.

That distinction would protect Amina from many future misunderstandings.

## Page 26: Why the Price Moves Every Minute

The exchange screen showed the Bitcoin price moving up and down.

One moment it was green.

A few seconds later it was red.

Amina stared at it.

“Who changes the price?”

“No single person changes it,” Kamau said. “The market price forms from buying and selling.”

At any moment, some people want to buy bitcoin. Others want to sell it.

Buyers place offers showing how much they are willing to pay.

Sellers place offers showing how much they are willing to accept.

These offers can appear in an **order book**.

Kamau drew a simple example.

### Buyers

- Buyer A wants 0.01 BTC at 9,900,000 shillings per BTC
- Buyer B wants 0.02 BTC at 9,850,000 shillings per BTC
- Buyer C wants 0.05 BTC at 9,800,000 shillings per BTC

### Sellers

- Seller A offers 0.01 BTC at 10,000,000 shillings per BTC
- Seller B offers 0.03 BTC at 10,050,000 shillings per BTC
- Seller C offers 0.05 BTC at 10,100,000 shillings per BTC

The highest buyer and the lowest seller are close, but they have not yet agreed.

When a buyer accepts a seller’s price, or a seller accepts a buyer’s price, a trade happens.

The latest completed trades help form the displayed market price.

Amina asked why Bitcoin could rise or fall so sharply.

Kamau listed several reasons:

- new buyers entering the market,
- large holders buying or selling,
- fear or excitement,
- government announcements,
- exchange failures,
- changes in interest rates,
- global economic events,
- new technology,
- rumours,
- and traders using borrowed money.

Crypto markets operate across many countries and often trade all day and all night.

Prices can react quickly.

Amina had seen people online saying, “Bitcoin is going up because more people believe in it.”

That could be partly true, but markets are rarely explained by one sentence.

Price is affected by supply and demand.

Bitcoin’s total supply rule may be limited, but the amount available for sale at a particular moment can still change.

Demand can also change suddenly.

If many people urgently want to buy and few want to sell, the price may rise.

If many urgently want to sell and few want to buy, the price may fall.

This movement is called **volatility**.

Volatility means the price changes significantly and sometimes quickly.

Amina imagined saving school fees in an asset that could fall sharply before the payment date.

“That would be dangerous,” she said.

“Yes. An asset may have long-term potential and still be unsuitable for money you need next week.”

This was an important financial lesson.

The purpose of money matters.

Emergency money should be available when an emergency comes.

Rent money should still be there when rent is due.

A person should not confuse a risky investment with safe short-term savings.

Amina wrote:

> A rising price does not prove an asset is safe. A falling price does not automatically prove the technology is useless.

Technology and market price are connected, but they are not the same thing.

## Page 27: Market Orders, Limit Orders, and the Tomato Stall

At the market, Amina stopped beside a tomato seller.

“How much for one kilogram?” she asked.

“Two hundred shillings.”

Amina needed tomatoes immediately, so she paid the current price.

Later, Kamau used the example to explain a **market order**.

A market order tells an exchange:

**Buy or sell now at the best available prices.**

It values speed more than a specific price.

If the market is active and the amount is small, the final price may be close to what the screen showed.

But in a thin market, or with a large order, the price can change while the order is being filled.

This difference is called **slippage**.

Suppose Amina wants to buy a large amount of a small token.

The first units may cost 10 shillings each.

After those sellers are used up, the next available units may cost 11, then 12, then 14 shillings.

Her average purchase price may become higher than the first price she saw.

A **limit order** works differently.

It says:

**Only buy at this price or lower.**

Or:

**Only sell at this price or higher.**

Amina could tell the tomato seller, “I will buy ten kilograms, but only if the price falls to 170 shillings per kilogram.”

The seller may agree later, or may never agree.

A limit order gives more price control, but it may not be completed.

Amina asked about the large green and red candles on trading charts.

Kamau explained that each candle summarises price movement during a chosen period.

A candle can show:

- the opening price,
- the closing price,
- the highest price,
- and the lowest price.

Charts can help people study market history, but they do not reveal the future with certainty.

Many beginners believe a chart pattern is a promise.

It is not.

Experienced traders can still be wrong.

Amina then saw a button marked **Leverage**.

Kamau immediately warned her.

Leverage allows a trader to control a larger position using borrowed funds or collateral.

It can multiply profits.

It can also multiply losses.

If the market moves too far against the position, the platform may close it automatically. This is called **liquidation**.

Amina imagined buying tomatoes with borrowed money, hoping the price would rise before repayment was due.

If the price fell, she could lose her own money and still face costs.

“Why would a beginner use leverage?” she asked.

“Many should not,” Kamau said.

The exchange app made advanced products look like ordinary buttons.

But a simple interface does not make a risky product safe.

Amina added a rule to her notebook:

> Never use a financial tool only because the button is available.

Understanding must come before action.

## Page 28: The Digital Dollar That Tries to Stay Still

Amina understood why people might buy bitcoin.

But one problem bothered her.

“If the price moves so much, how do traders keep money inside crypto markets without always returning to a bank?”

Kamau introduced her to **stablecoins**.

A stablecoin is a crypto token designed to keep a relatively stable value compared with another asset.

Many stablecoins try to stay close to one United States dollar.

Examples may trade under names such as USDT or USDC.

Amina asked, “Is a stablecoin just a digital dollar?”

“Not exactly. It is a token that tries to match the value of a dollar. The design and risks depend on how it is issued.”

Some stablecoins are issued by companies.

The company may hold cash, bank deposits, government securities, or other assets intended to support the tokens in circulation.

If one token is meant to equal one dollar, users expect the issuer to have enough reliable assets and redemption arrangements.

Other stablecoins use crypto assets as collateral.

Some use algorithms and market incentives.

These designs can have very different risks.

Amina compared stablecoins with mobile money.

Both can show digital balances.

Both can be sent using electronic systems.

But mobile money in Kenya is normally denominated directly in Kenyan shillings and managed by a licensed service provider within the national payment system.

A dollar stablecoin is a token on a blockchain or related network. It depends on the issuer, reserves, smart contracts, banks, custodians, and the network it uses.

Stablecoins can be useful for:

- moving value between exchanges,
- international payments,
- saving in a dollar-linked unit,
- paying online workers,
- using decentralised finance,
- and settling transactions at any hour.

But “stable” does not mean risk-free.

A stablecoin can lose its target value. This is called losing its **peg**.

The issuer may face legal action.

Bank accounts holding reserves may be frozen.

A smart contract may fail.

A blockchain may become congested.

The issuer may have weak or unclear reserves.

A user may send the token on the wrong network.

Some stablecoin issuers can freeze particular addresses.

This surprised Amina.

“I thought blockchain money could not be frozen.”

“Some assets are designed with central control,” Kamau explained. “Crypto is a broad category. Not every token has Bitcoin’s design.”

A stablecoin can run on a public blockchain while still having a central company that issues it.

Decentralised rails do not automatically make the asset itself decentralised.

Amina wrote three questions to ask before using a stablecoin:

1. Who issues it?
2. What supports its value?
3. Can it be redeemed, frozen, or changed?

She added one more:

4. Which network is the token on?

The same stablecoin name may appear on several networks.

Sending it through the wrong network can cause delays or permanent loss.

Stablecoins looked simple because their prices tried to stay still.

Behind that stillness was a complicated system of trust.

## Page 29: Meeting Ethereum

One evening, Kevin asked Amina why people talked about Ethereum when Bitcoin already existed.

Amina took the question to Kamau.

“Bitcoin was designed mainly around digital money and secure value transfer,” he said. “Ethereum expanded the idea of a blockchain into a general platform for programs.”

Ethereum is a blockchain network that can run **smart contracts**.

A smart contract is a program stored and executed according to the rules of a blockchain.

The name can be confusing.

A smart contract is not always a legal contract.

It is not necessarily smart.

It is code that follows programmed instructions.

Kamau gave Amina an example using a chama.

Suppose ten members contribute money every month.

The group creates a rule:

- when all ten contributions arrive,
- send the pooled amount to the selected member,
- record the payment,
- then prepare for the next month.

A normal computer system could automate this.

A smart contract tries to place the program on a blockchain so participants can inspect and interact with shared rules.

This can reduce dependence on one company’s private database.

But it does not remove every form of trust.

Users may still trust:

- the developers who wrote the code,
- auditors who reviewed it,
- external data sources,
- the wallet interface,
- administrators with special powers,
- and the blockchain network itself.

Ethereum has its own native asset called **ether**, often written as ETH.

Ether is used to pay for activity on the network.

It may also be held, traded, or used in network security.

Amina asked whether ETH was simply another version of bitcoin.

“No. They are separate assets on separate networks with different histories, designs, communities, and goals.”

Bitcoin and Ethereum are often compared, but the comparison can be misleading if people assume one must completely replace the other.

Bitcoin focuses strongly on scarcity, security, and a conservative monetary system.

Ethereum focuses strongly on programmable applications and a flexible smart contract environment.

Both have changed over time, but they make different trade-offs.

Amina imagined Bitcoin as a highly protected digital settlement book.

She imagined Ethereum as a shared computer where many people could run programs.

The comparison was simplified, but helpful.

She wrote:

> Bitcoin showed that digital value could be transferred without one central ledger keeper. Ethereum showed that shared blockchain rules could also run programmable applications.

The world of crypto was no longer only about coins.

It was also about software that could control coins.

## Page 30: Gas Fees and the Busy Highway

Amina tried a learning application on Ethereum.

Before she could complete an action, the wallet showed a fee.

“Why am I paying when no person is serving me?” she asked.

Kamau explained **gas**.

On Ethereum, gas is a way of measuring the computational work needed to process an action.

A simple transfer may need less work than a complicated smart contract interaction.

The user pays a network fee using ETH.

The fee helps compensate network participants and prevents unlimited free computation.

Without fees, attackers could flood the network with useless work.

Kamau compared the network to a highway.

When few cars are travelling, the road has space.

When many drivers want to use the same road, congestion grows.

On a blockchain, limited block space means users may compete to have transactions included.

Fees can rise when demand is high.

Amina asked why a failed transaction could still cost money.

“The network may still have performed work before the action failed.”

Imagine paying a mechanic to inspect a car. Even if the car cannot be repaired, the inspection still used time and effort.

The comparison was not exact, but it helped.

A smart contract transaction can fail because:

- the user does not have enough funds,
- the allowed price changed,
- the contract rejected the action,
- the gas limit was too low,
- or the application had a problem.

Fees are one reason blockchains face a difficult challenge called **scalability**.

A network wants to be:

- secure,
- decentralised,
- and able to process many transactions cheaply.

Improving one area can create pressure in another.

Some networks increase block capacity or reduce the number of validators.

This may improve speed, but can make it harder for ordinary users to verify the system.

Ethereum also uses additional networks and systems called **Layer 2 solutions**.

A Layer 2 can process many activities away from the main Ethereum chain, then send compressed results or proofs back to it.

Kamau compared this to a supermarket.

Instead of every customer paying individually at one slow counter, several smaller counters process groups of customers and later send totals to the main office.

Layer 2 systems can reduce costs and increase capacity.

But they introduce their own risks, rules, bridges, and technical designs.

Amina realised that “faster and cheaper” was not the end of the discussion.

She needed to ask:

- Who operates the system?
- Can users withdraw without permission?
- What happens if the operator fails?
- Where is the final record settled?
- What security assumptions are being made?

A cheap transaction is useful.

A cheap transaction on an unsafe system may be expensive in the end.

## Page 31: Tokens, Like Digital Vouchers With Extra Powers

At a supermarket, Amina received a paper voucher worth 500 shillings.

It could be used only in that supermarket.

It looked like money, but it was not the same as the Kenyan shilling.

Kamau used the voucher to explain **tokens**.

A crypto token is a digital asset created on an existing blockchain platform.

Ethereum, for example, allows developers to create tokens through smart contracts.

The network’s native asset is ETH.

A token created on Ethereum is not the same thing as ETH, even though it uses Ethereum’s network.

Tokens can represent many things:

- access to a service,
- voting rights in a project,
- a stablecoin,
- a game item,
- a claim on another asset,
- a reward point,
- or a speculative investment.

Amina asked whether anyone could create a token.

“In many systems, yes. Creating a token can be technically easy.”

That answer worried her.

If creation is easy, quality cannot be assumed.

A token may have a professional website, a large social media following, and an exciting name while still having little real value.

Before buying a token, Amina should ask:

1. What does it actually do?
2. Who created it?
3. How many tokens exist?
4. Who owns most of them?
5. Can more be created?
6. Can the contract be changed?
7. Is there real demand beyond speculation?
8. Can insiders sell large amounts?
9. Has the code been reviewed?
10. What happens if the project fails?

Kamau explained **token supply**.

A project may have:

- circulating supply,
- total supply,
- and maximum supply.

Circulating supply is the amount currently available in the market.

Total supply includes tokens that exist but may not be circulating.

Maximum supply is the highest amount allowed, if the design has a fixed limit.

Amina saw one token priced at only 2 shillings.

“It is cheap,” she said.

“Price per token alone tells you very little.”

A project can create billions of tokens.

A token priced at 2 shillings may represent a larger total valuation than another token priced at 20,000 shillings.

To compare projects, people often look at **market capitalisation**.

Market capitalisation is roughly:

**token price × circulating supply**

But even market capitalisation is not a perfect measure.

Thin trading, locked tokens, insider ownership, and future token releases can change the picture.

Amina wrote:

> A low token price does not mean a token is cheap. It may simply mean there are many units.

Numbers can look friendly while hiding large risks.

## Page 32: The Bridge Across Two Blockchains

Amina now had a small stablecoin balance on one network.

She found an application that required the same stablecoin on another network.

“Can I just send it there?” she asked.

“Not directly unless the receiving service supports that exact network,” Kamau replied.

Different blockchains are like separate islands.

Each island has its own roads, rules, records, and native assets.

A token on one chain does not automatically exist on another.

A **blockchain bridge** connects value or messages between networks.

A common bridge design works like this:

1. A user sends or locks an asset on Chain A.
2. The bridge records that event.
3. A related token is issued or released on Chain B.
4. When the user returns, the Chain B version is destroyed or locked.
5. The original asset is released on Chain A.

Amina compared it to leaving Kenyan shillings with a trusted money changer and receiving an equivalent claim in another form.

The claim is useful only if the bridge continues to honour it.

Bridges can depend on:

- smart contracts,
- groups of signers,
- validators,
- price or message oracles,
- and administrators.

If one of these parts fails, users may lose funds.

Bridges have been targets of major attacks because they can hold large amounts of value.

Amina asked whether a bridged token was the same as the original.

Economically, people may treat it as equivalent.

Technically, it may be a separate token representing a claim.

That claim carries bridge risk.

This taught Amina another broad rule:

> Every extra layer of convenience can introduce another layer of trust.

She also learned about **wrapped tokens**.

A wrapped token represents an asset in a form that another network or smart contract can use.

For example, a token may represent bitcoin on a smart contract platform.

The holder must understand who or what keeps the connection between the wrapped token and the original bitcoin.

Amina began to see why crypto users sometimes lost money even when the main blockchain itself was still running correctly.

The failure could happen in:

- a bridge,
- an exchange,
- a wallet,
- a smart contract,
- a price feed,
- or a user’s own decision.

“The blockchain worked” does not mean every application on it worked safely.

## Page 33: The Chama Without a Treasurer

At the next chama meeting, members discussed where to keep a small emergency fund.

Amina imagined a program that followed the group’s rules automatically.

No single treasurer could withdraw alone.

Every member could see the balance.

Loans could follow agreed conditions.

Kamau said, “You are now imagining decentralised finance.”

**Decentralised finance**, usually called **DeFi**, refers to financial services built with blockchain smart contracts.

DeFi applications may allow people to:

- exchange tokens,
- lend assets,
- borrow assets,
- earn fees,
- create stablecoins,
- buy insurance-like protection,
- or manage shared funds.

Users often interact through a wallet instead of opening a traditional account.

The smart contract follows programmed rules.

This can make services available at any hour and across borders.

But DeFi is not automatically decentralised in every part.

A project may have a central website.

Developers may control upgrade keys.

A small group may hold most voting tokens.

External companies may provide price data.

Stablecoins used in the application may be centrally issued.

The word decentralised should therefore be investigated, not simply believed.

Amina asked how DeFi lending works without a loan officer.

Many DeFi loans require the borrower to deposit crypto collateral worth more than the loan.

For example, a borrower may lock crypto worth 150,000 shillings to borrow stablecoins worth 100,000 shillings.

If the collateral value falls too far, the system may sell it automatically to protect lenders.

This is called **liquidation**.

Amina frowned.

“That would not help someone who has no assets.”

“Correct. Many DeFi loans are not like ordinary personal loans. They are often tools for people who already own crypto.”

DeFi removes some gatekeepers, but it does not remove economic reality.

Lenders still want protection.

Borrowers still face costs.

Risk still exists.

Amina wrote a list of DeFi risks:

- smart contract bugs,
- unstable collateral,
- liquidation,
- fake applications,
- stolen private keys,
- manipulated prices,
- central administrator powers,
- and changing returns.

She also added a human risk:

- not understanding the product.

A high advertised return can attract users.

But yield must come from somewhere.

It may come from trading fees, borrower interest, token rewards, inflation, or new users entering.

If the source is unclear, the return should not be trusted blindly.

## Page 34: The Automated Market Stall

Amina understood a normal exchange with an order book.

Buyers and sellers placed offers.

But a DeFi application showed no visible list of buyers and sellers.

“How does trading happen here?”

Kamau explained an **automated market maker**, or **AMM**.

An AMM uses a smart contract and pools of tokens to support trading.

A **liquidity pool** may hold two assets, such as ETH and a stablecoin.

Users trade against the pool.

A formula changes the price as the balance of the assets changes.

Amina imagined a market stall with two baskets.

One basket held mangoes.

The other held oranges.

If many people took mangoes and added oranges, mangoes became scarcer in the stall. The stall’s formula would make mangoes more expensive relative to oranges.

People called **liquidity providers** place assets into the pool.

In return, they may receive part of the trading fees.

This sounded attractive to Amina.

“Can I earn without doing anything?”

“You are providing assets and taking risks. That is not nothing.”

Liquidity providers may face **impermanent loss**.

The name sounds temporary, but the loss can become real when funds are withdrawn.

Suppose Amina deposits equal values of two tokens.

If one token’s market price changes greatly, the AMM automatically adjusts the amounts inside the pool.

When Amina withdraws, she may receive a different combination than she deposited.

She may end up with less value than if she had simply held the two assets separately.

Trading fees can reduce or sometimes overcome that difference, but there is no guarantee.

Pools also face:

- smart contract risk,
- token price collapse,
- fake token risk,
- low trading activity,
- and sudden withdrawal of liquidity.

A pool with very little liquidity can have large slippage.

A scammer may create a token, attract buyers, then remove the paired assets. This is often called a **rug pull**.

Amina imagined a market owner inviting everyone to trade, then carrying away the cash box at night.

The technology was new.

The behaviour was old.

Greed, deception, and poor judgment did not disappear on a blockchain.

Amina wrote:

> A smart contract can automate rules. It cannot guarantee that the rules are fair, useful, or safe.

Code follows instructions.

The difficult question is who wrote the instructions and what they allow.

## Page 35: A Picture, a Receipt, or a Membership Card?

Kevin showed Amina a colourful digital picture.

“The owner paid a lot of money for this NFT,” he said.

Amina looked confused.

“Why pay for a picture that I can screenshot?”

Kamau explained that an **NFT**, or non-fungible token, is a unique or individually identifiable token recorded on a blockchain.

“Non-fungible” means the units are not treated as perfectly interchangeable.

One 100-shilling note is usually treated like another 100-shilling note of the same value.

But one land title is not interchangeable with another. Each refers to a different property.

An NFT can act like a blockchain record connected to a particular item, right, or identifier.

It may represent:

- digital artwork,
- a game item,
- an event ticket,
- membership access,
- a certificate,
- a collectible,
- or another unique record.

The NFT itself does not always contain the image.

It may contain a link pointing to a file stored somewhere else.

If that storage disappears, the token may remain while the linked media becomes unavailable.

Owning an NFT also does not automatically give copyright ownership.

A person may own the token but not the legal right to reproduce the artwork commercially.

The exact rights depend on the project’s terms and applicable law.

Amina returned to the screenshot question.

A screenshot copies the visible image.

It does not copy the blockchain record or the wallet history connected to the NFT.

Kamau compared it to photographing a concert ticket.

The photograph may look like the ticket, but the venue’s system may recognise only the valid ticket record.

However, value still depends on demand and usefulness.

An NFT can become worthless if nobody wants it or if the promised benefits disappear.

Some NFT projects have supported artists and communities.

Others have used hype, fake scarcity, wash trading, and misleading promises.

**Wash trading** happens when someone trades an asset between related accounts to create a false appearance of activity or value.

Amina realised that public blockchain data did not always reveal the true story immediately.

Several addresses may look like different people while being controlled by one person.

NFTs taught her a wider lesson:

> A blockchain can prove that a token moved between addresses. It cannot automatically prove that the price was fair, the buyer was independent, or the promise was honest.

Data needs interpretation.

## Page 36: The Day a Scammer Called Amina

Amina received a phone call from a man who claimed to work for a crypto exchange.

“We noticed a problem with your wallet,” he said. “You must verify your account immediately.”

He sent her a link.

The page looked professional.

It had a logo, customer reviews, and a countdown timer.

Then the man asked for her seed phrase.

Amina’s heart began to beat faster.

She remembered Page 17.

A seed phrase is not a password to share with support staff.

It is a master recovery secret.

Anyone who receives it may be able to control the wallet.

Amina ended the call.

She did not click the link.

She contacted the exchange through its official app and confirmed that no employee had called her.

Kamau later explained common crypto scams.

### Fake support

Scammers pretend to be exchange or wallet employees. They ask for passwords, codes, private keys, seed phrases, or remote access to a device.

### Phishing

A fake website or message copies a real service and tricks users into entering secrets or approving transactions.

### Guaranteed returns

A person promises fixed daily profits, risk-free trading, or secret investment systems.

Real investments do not offer guaranteed high returns without risk.

### Romance and friendship scams

A scammer builds trust over time, then introduces a fake investment platform or urgent financial problem.

### Impersonation

A scammer copies the identity of a celebrity, government official, company, friend, or family member.

### Fake tokens and rug pulls

Creators sell a token, control most of the supply, remove liquidity, or disappear.

### Malicious wallet approvals

A user connects a wallet to a dangerous application and approves permission for assets to be moved.

### Recovery scams

After someone loses money, another scammer promises to recover it for an advance fee.

Amina created a safety routine.

Before sending or approving anything, she would stop and ask:

1. Did I begin this contact, or did a stranger contact me?
2. Am I being rushed?
3. Is anyone asking for my seed phrase or private key?
4. Is the website address correct?
5. Can I verify the request through an official channel?
6. What permission am I signing?
7. Can I test with a tiny amount first?
8. What happens if I am wrong?

She also separated her funds.

A small everyday wallet could interact with applications.

A more protected wallet could hold long-term savings.

She would not keep every asset in one place.

She enabled strong account security and avoided reusing passwords.

She kept recovery information offline and private.

Most importantly, she accepted that there was no shame in slowing down.

Scammers create urgency because careful thinking is their enemy.

Amina closed her notebook and wrote the final lesson of Part Three:

> In crypto, control moves closer to the user. So does responsibility.

She now understood exchanges, market prices, orders, stablecoins, Ethereum, smart contracts, gas, tokens, bridges, DeFi, liquidity pools, NFTs, and common scams.

The next part would go deeper.

She would study proof of stake, validators, governance, DAOs, oracles, token economics, privacy, scaling, regulation, and how to judge whether a crypto project solves a real problem.

# Learning Checkpoint Three

### 1. What is a crypto exchange?

A service or marketplace where users buy, sell, or trade crypto assets. It is separate from the blockchain itself.

### 2. Why do crypto prices move?

Prices change as buyers and sellers change their offers, expectations, and urgency.

### 3. What is a stablecoin?

A token designed to maintain a relatively stable value compared with another asset, often the United States dollar.

### 4. What is Ethereum?

A blockchain network designed to support programmable smart contracts and applications.

### 5. What is gas?

A measure of computational work used to calculate fees for Ethereum transactions and smart contract actions.

### 6. What is a token?

A digital asset created using an existing blockchain or smart contract platform.

### 7. What is DeFi?

Financial services built using blockchain smart contracts, such as trading, lending, and borrowing.

### 8. What is a liquidity pool?

A smart contract holding tokens that users can trade against, often funded by liquidity providers.

### 9. What is an NFT?

A unique or individually identifiable blockchain token that may represent a digital item, right, membership, or record.

### 10. What should never be shared?

A private key or seed phrase.
